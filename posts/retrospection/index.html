Retrospection.

It has been almost two years since I wrote my last blog and a lot has changed in my life in that time. I wrote my last blog before joining the University of California San Diego for my graduate degree, and as of today it has almost been 2 months since I finished my studies and graduated. This blog is a closure for that journey and I am writing this to note down a few key details people often miss.

Before I joined my master’s at UC San Diego, I had no idea of how to get interships or jobs, and how to fund my education through Teaching Assistant-ships (TA-ships) and Research positions. A few highly proactive students (proactive, not smart) among ourselves who had the right guidance had secured internships in FAANG even before the program started and had secured funding oppurtunites to pay for their educations. Unfortunately, I wasn’t one of them, and because of that I ended up drilling a hole in my parents’ pockets. However, that was a good experience for me to learn the importance of being proactive and the importance of people skill.

Being Proactive is More Rewarding than Being Smart#

Don’t get me wrong, being smart is very important. Being good at what you do will take you a long way in life. But just being smart will not fetch you any good oppurtunities. You have to be proactive as well.

For example, I thought filling the online TA application form was good enough to get me a TA position in the university. And in my mind that was going to work because I am smart and the university will know that when they read my application. WRONG. In the second quarter of my masters’ I realized that filling the application does not help at all. What makes difference is reaching out to the professors to express your interest in TA-ing for their course. Those of us who knew this secret ended up getting TA-ships way earlier than the rest of us and were happy for getting half of their tuition waived off.

I followed the same approach and reached out to professors like crazy and luckily landed a TA-ship in the third quarter of my master’s. The university waived off half of my tuition, paid for my insurance, and paid me a monthly stipend. I was happy.

Lesson learnt: No matter how smart you are, you need to make yourself visible for someone to notice you.

Being Proactive is Important, but Luck Plays a Very Big Role Too#

There are two main ways to fund your education - through Teaching Assistantships (TAships) or Research Assistantships (RAships). They both get you a monthly stipend in almost all universities, but in a few well known universities they help you waive off certain portions of your tuition as well. For instance, at UC San Diego, TAships waives off half of the tution while RAships completely waives it off. However, RAships are significantly harder to secure.

Back when I joined UC San Diego, I never even imagined getting a RAship because they seemed a bit out of my league. Interstingly, I came third in this one course I took in the second quarter and the professor asked me to join his lab in the next quarter. I never expected that he will fund my education, but turns out he really liked my performance and decided to fund the rest of my education. Usually students carefully approach professors to get a funded position in their lab. But here I was, didn’t reach out to a single professor, yet this wonderful oppurtunity landed in my laps and I never paid a single penny in the rest of my master’s. Infact, I ended up saving enough to break even the dollars I spent in the first two quarters - effectively making my education completely free!

Lesson learnt: It is important to be proactive and put yourself out there, but luck plays a major role. Unfortunately, luck is one of those things that you have no control over. It sometimes work, but mostly it doesn’t.
